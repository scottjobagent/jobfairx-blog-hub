Recruiting Metrics & ROI

Cost Per Hire: What It Is, 2026 Benchmarks by Industry, and How to Lower It

By Scott Lobenberg, Founder, JobFairX  ·  Published July 2026

Hiring someone costs a third more than it did a decade ago. The average U.S. cost per hire is now $5,475 for non-executive roles, up 33% since 2016, per SHRM's 2025 Benchmarking Report. In healthcare, expect $9,000–$12,000. Executives run $35,879.

Most teams don't know their number, and the ones that do usually undercount it. This guide covers the formula, 2026 benchmarks by industry, and six ways to bring the number down.

What is cost per hire?

Cost per hire is the total amount you spend on recruiting divided by the number of people you actually hire. It's the efficiency metric for a recruiting budget: one number that says what it costs you to land a new employee, and whether that cost is moving the right way.

How do you calculate cost per hire?

The SHRM formula:

Cost per hire = (internal recruiting costs + external recruiting costs) ÷ total number of hires

Internal costs: recruiter salaries (allocated to hiring time), interviewer hours, referral bonuses. External costs: job boards, ads, agencies, recruiting software, background checks, career events.

For a typical mid-size company, the money breaks down roughly like this: recruiter time 30–40%, job boards and advertising 20–25%, software 10–15%, interviewer time 10–15%, background checks and assessments 5–10%.

Two things most teams get wrong when they run the formula. They forget interviewer time: hours your managers spend in interviews are a real cost, and for hourly roles with high interview volume they're often the biggest one. And they divide by offers instead of hires. Ghosted offers and no-shows inflate your true cost per hire, which is why the metric pairs with time to fill.

Worth knowing: these are direct-cost figures. Research from the Chartered Institute of Personnel and Development (CIPD) and the Recruitment Process Outsourcing Association (RPOA) puts the fully loaded number, with hiring-manager time and lost productivity counted, at roughly double the invoice total.

And cost per hire isn't even the expensive part in some industries. In healthcare, the vacancy is. The 2026 NSI Retention Report puts the average cost of a single RN turnover at $60,090, with experienced RN roles taking 56–102 days to fill. When the empty seat costs 6x the recruiting spend, slow-and-cheap hiring is the most expensive strategy there is.

Why is cost per hire going up?

Three reasons, mostly.

Job board economics. Pay-per-click pricing keeps rising while application quality doesn't. You're paying for clicks and applications, not interviews and not hires. When 100 applications produce 4 interviews, your cost per interview is 25x your cost per click, and nobody puts that number in the dashboard.

Recruiter time on screening. Most of the 30–40% of cost that's recruiter salary gets burned on reviewing resumes and chasing candidates who never respond. That's the "post and pray" tax.

Ghosting and no-shows. Every interview that doesn't happen and every offer that gets ghosted pushes spend onto fewer hires. Same numerator, smaller denominator.

6 ways to lower cost per hire

  1. Measure cost per interview, not cost per application. Applications are an input. Interviews are the first unit that predicts a hire. Re-run last quarter's numbers as spend ÷ interviews-that-happened and you'll find out which channels are actually expensive.
  2. Pay flat rates instead of per-click where you can. Variable-cost channels punish volume hiring. Flat-rate channels, including hiring events, mean your cost per hire drops as you fill more roles from one spend.

  3. Batch interviews into events. One recruiter running back-to-back scheduled interviews for a day beats three weeks of calendar Tetris. This is the single biggest lever for high-volume hiring. Interviewer time is your largest hidden cost, and batching cuts it hard.
  4. Only interview people who asked to meet you. No-shows collapse when the candidate requested the interview slot instead of being chased into it. Pre-qualified, self-scheduled interviews flip the dynamic: candidates who picked your company and picked the time show up.

  5. Cut your time to fill. Cost per hire and time to fill move together. Every extra week a role sits open adds recruiter hours, ad spend, and (in healthcare especially) vacancy cost that dwarfs the recruiting budget.
  6. Actually calculate channel ROI. Most teams can't say what a hire from each channel costs because the spend is pooled. Separate the data per channel, per quarter. Our recruiting ROI guide has the formulas and a business-case template.

Where do hiring events fit?

Worth a straight answer, since we run them. Flat-rate hiring events are one of the few channels where cost per hire falls as you hire more, because the spend is fixed while the hires aren't. That's the flat-rate effect from #2, and it's why events tend to win for multi-role, hourly, and clinical hiring, where interview volume is the bottleneck.

The honest caveat: an event is not the right tool for every role. A single specialized senior hire is still a sourcing-and-outreach game. If you want the actual event math against these benchmarks, we published it on our hiring event pricing page.

FAQ

What is a good cost per hire?

Below your industry benchmark, trending down. For hourly and entry-level roles, under $3,000. Against the $5,475 U.S. average, anything under ~$2,000 is strong. But a "good" number with a 90-day time to fill isn't good. Read the two metrics together.

What should be included in cost per hire?

Everything recruiting touches: recruiter and sourcer compensation (allocated), job boards, advertising, agencies, software, background checks, referral bonuses, interviewer time, and events. SHRM's formula: total internal + external recruiting costs ÷ total hires.

Why is healthcare cost per hire so high?

Credentialing, licensure verification, a shrinking candidate pool, and competition. Healthcare hires cost $9,000–$12,000 and health services roles take the longest to fill of any industry (49 days per Workable). The vacancy cost makes it worse: NSI puts one RN turnover at $60,090.

Does cost per hire include onboarding?

Under the SHRM standard, no. It stops at the accepted offer. Some teams track "cost per productive hire" including onboarding and ramp; useful, but keep it separate so your benchmark comparisons stay apples-to-apples.

Healthcare Hiring

Why Hospitals Struggle to Fill Entry-Level Clinical Roles (and What Actually Works)

By Scott Lobenberg, Founder, JobFairX  ·  Published July 2026

Hospitals struggle to fill entry-level clinical roles for a simple reason: the hiring process was built for a world where candidates waited around. They don't anymore. The average hospital takes 49 days to fill a role (the slowest of any industry) while entry-level candidates accept the first decent offer they get, usually within days. Add a 9.6% national RN vacancy rate and a recruiting model that collects applications instead of scheduling interviews, and you get the gap every hospital talent team knows too well.

Here are the seven failure modes we see most, and what actually fixes each one.

Why is healthcare hiring so slow?

Start with the scoreboard:

Health services roles take 49 days to fill on average (Workable), and NSI's 2026 Retention Report puts the average recruiting time for an experienced RN at 78 days, with a range of 56 to 102. Meanwhile every open seat bleeds money: one RN turnover costs $60,090, and the average hospital loses $5.19 million a year to nurse turnover alone.

That's the backdrop. Now the seven reasons.

1. The funnel is optimized for applications, not interviews

Most hospital career sites and job board postings are built to collect applications. But an application isn't a candidate. It's a resume in a queue. For entry-level roles, the queue is huge (everyone applies to everything) and the signal is thin. Recruiters drown in volume while the few genuinely interested candidates sit unanswered.

What works: flip the unit of progress from applications to scheduled interviews. If a channel can't put a qualified candidate on your calendar, it's a resume collector. Measure interviews per week per open req. It's the number that predicts fills.

2. Speed kills, and hospitals are slow

Entry-level clinical candidates (CNAs, medical assistants, new grad nurses) are often juggling multiple offers within a week of starting their search. A 49-day process isn't competing with other hospitals; it's competing with the retail distribution center that made an offer on Thursday.

What works: compress the top of the funnel. The event model does this structurally: candidates are matched, screened, and sitting in scheduled interviews on a single day, instead of moving through a six-stage pipeline over two months. (More on how that works in our guide to virtual hiring events.)

3. Traditional career fairs collect resumes, not hires

The classic hospital job fair (booth, banner, bowl of pens) is a branding exercise. NACE data says only 45% of job fair attendees get so much as an interview invitation, and 24% an offer, and most of those interviews happen weeks later, after the momentum is gone. You paid for a day of your recruiters' time and walked away with a stack of resumes you could have gotten from a job board.

What works: events built around interviews instead of foot traffic. If the format doesn't put candidates and hiring managers in actual scheduled interviews that day, it's marketing, not recruiting. Here's how to run interviews at a virtual hiring event so those slots convert.

4. No-shows and ghosting eat the calendar

Every talent team has the story: 20 interviews scheduled, 9 people show. Entry-level roles ghost hardest. The candidate who applied to 40 jobs doesn't remember yours. The hidden cost is enormous: hiring manager hours reserved and burned, and your cost per hire quietly doubles because the denominator shrinks.

What works: self-scheduled interviews. When the candidate picks your hospital and picks the time slot, rather than being chased into one by a recruiter, show rates change fundamentally. Requested interviews get honored; assigned ones get ghosted.

5. Credentialing bottlenecks stall qualified candidates

Licensure verification, certifications, background checks: all necessary, but hospitals often run them sequentially after the interview stage, adding weeks. The candidate who aced the interview in week 3 has accepted elsewhere by week 6.

What works: screen for credentials before the interview, not after. Ask licensure and certification questions at registration so everyone your team meets is already interview-ready, and run verification in parallel with the offer process, not after it.

6. New grads are evaluated like experienced hires

New grad nurses and allied health students get filtered by experience requirements they can't possibly meet, interviewed with questions designed for five-year veterans, and offered roles with no visible growth path. Then hospitals wonder why early-career pipelines are thin while turnover keeps the front door revolving (16.4% RN turnover nationally).

What works: separate entry-level tracks: dedicated entry-level events, interview rubrics built for potential over experience, and residency/preceptor programs mentioned in the recruiting conversation, not discovered after the start date.

7. Nobody owns the outcome

Postings are HR's job, interviews are the unit manager's job, and the vacancy is everyone's problem, so it's no one's. Weeks pass between a promising screen and a manager interview because two calendars never met.

What works: one owner per req with authority over scheduling, and formats that force calendar convergence. An event day does this by design: managers block the day, candidates fill the slots, decisions happen while everyone's still in the room.

How do hiring events reduce nurse vacancies?

They attack the three failure modes that matter most at entry level: speed (interviews happen in days, not weeks; Indeed's event data shows a median of 4 days from signup to interview), show rates (self-scheduled interviews vs. chased ones), and volume (dozens of pre-screened candidates in one day instead of a trickle over two months). They don't fix retention, credentialing policy, or pay bands. No recruiting tool does. But for getting qualified entry-level candidates in front of hiring managers before the market takes them, nothing else compresses the timeline as hard.

FAQ

Why do hospitals struggle with virtual job fairs?

Usually because they run them like virtual versions of the booth-and-banner fair: a lobby, some chat, no scheduled interviews. The format isn't the problem; the missing interview structure is. Virtual events work when every attendee is matched, screened, and booked into a time slot. See our breakdown of virtual vs. in-person hiring events.

What makes it hard to fill clinical roles through job fairs?

Three things: generic audiences (a general job fair delivers few credentialed clinical candidates), no pre-screening (managers interview unqualified walk-ins), and no same-day interviews (resumes go in a pile and momentum dies). Fix those three and the format works.

How much does an unfilled nursing position cost?

NSI's 2026 report puts RN turnover cost at $60,090 per nurse, with the average hospital losing $4.2–6.2 million annually. Every 1-point change in RN turnover costs or saves about $295,000 a year.

What's the fastest way to hire entry-level clinical staff?

Compress screening and interviewing into a single event day. Candidates matched to your roles self-schedule interviews, your team meets 20–100+ pre-qualified candidates in one day, and offers go out while candidates are still engaged.

How to Choose a Nurse Job Fair: A Hospital Hiring Guide

By Scott Lobenberg, Founder, JobFairX  ·  Published July 2026

Choosing a nurse job fair comes down to seven questions, and the first one does most of the work: does the event deliver scheduled interviews with credential-screened candidates, or a room full of foot traffic? Everything else (specialty coverage, multi-location support, cost structure, reporting) matters, but an event that doesn't put nurses on your interview calendar is a branding exercise, not a recruiting channel.

Here's the full evaluation checklist, the honest comparison of event types, and what large hospital systems actually do.

The 7-point checklist for evaluating a nurse job fair

  1. Pre-screening before the interview. Ask exactly what candidates are asked at registration. Licensure? Certifications? Experience level? Location and shift preferences? If the answer is "they just sign up," your hiring managers will spend the day interviewing people who can't take the job. Credential questions at registration are the difference between an interview day and a screening day.
  2. Scheduled interviews, not booths. The single biggest predictor of hires per event. Booth formats, physical or virtual, produce conversations and resumes. Interview formats produce decisions. NACE data on traditional career fairs says only 45% of attendees get even one interview invitation, and most of those happen weeks later. Ask the operator: how many scheduled interviews does my team walk in with?
  3. Nursing and allied coverage, not general audiences. A general job fair in your city will hand you retail and warehouse applicants with a few CNAs sprinkled in. Look for healthcare-dedicated events, and ask for the candidate mix from the operator's last three events: RN vs. LPN vs. CNA vs. allied vs. non-clinical.
  4. Show-rate accountability. Interview no-shows are where event ROI dies. Ask what the operator's average show rate is and, more revealing, how interviews get scheduled. Self-scheduled interviews (candidate picks your hospital and picks the slot) reliably outperform recruiter-chased scheduling. For calibration: JobFairX healthcare events average a 91% interview show rate. That's the self-scheduling effect in one number. If the operator doesn't track show rate at all, that's your answer.
  5. Multi-location support. If you're filling roles across facilities, one city-by-city event contract per location gets expensive and inconsistent. Ask how the platform handles multiple locations per event, and whether one recruiting team can interview for several facilities in a single day.
  6. Flat cost you can do math on. Per-click and per-applicant pricing punishes volume, exactly what nurse hiring needs. A flat per-event rate means your cost per hire drops with every additional fill. Whatever the model, get to one number: expected cost per scheduled interview. (Healthcare cost per hire runs $9,000–$12,000 through traditional channels, so the bar is low.)
  7. Post-event data. Resumes in a folder is not reporting. You want: interviews completed, dispositions per candidate, no-show list, and contact info for follow-ups, exportable the same day.

What do large hospital systems actually do?

The pattern we see across systems hiring at scale: a standing cadence, not one-off events. Monthly or bi-monthly specialty events per region, entry-level and experienced tracks separated, and one recruiting team interviewing for multiple facilities per event day. The cadence matters because nurse hiring is continuous. A single spring job fair against a 16.4% annual RN turnover rate is a bucket against a tide. Systems also anchor events to predictable spikes: new grad graduation windows, seasonal census, new unit openings.

Recruiting Metrics & ROI

10 Recruiting Metrics That Actually Matter in 2026

By Scott Lobenberg, Founder, JobFairX  ·  Published July 2026

Most recruiting dashboards track activity — postings live, applications received, resumes reviewed. None of those predict a hire. The metrics that matter measure conversion and speed: how fast candidates move, what each stage costs, and where your funnel leaks. Here are the ten worth tracking, with formulas and 2026 benchmarks for each.

For scale, the funnel you're managing looks like this:

Only 3% of applicants ever reach an interview, and roughly 1 in 180 gets hired (CareerPlug, 10M+ applications). In other words: 97% of applicants are eliminated before they speak to a human. Keep that in mind as you read — most of these metrics exist to find out where your 180 go.

1. Time to fill

What: days from req opened to offer accepted. Formula: offer-accept date − req-open date.
Benchmark: 46–63 days average across industries; hospitality 20.7, retail 24.6, IT 30, health services 49 (Workable).
Why it matters: every open-seat day costs coverage, overtime, and in clinical settings real money — one open RN role bleeds roughly $770–$1,070 a day. Full guide: time to fill.

2. Time to hire

What: days from a candidate's first touch to accepted offer — measures process speed, where time-to-fill measures demand-to-fill speed. Benchmark: 46.2 days average (Employ). Entry-level candidates decide in days; if your time to hire is 40+, you're hiring from the leftovers.

3. Cost per hire

What: all recruiting spend ÷ hires. Benchmark: $5,475 average non-executive; $9,000–$12,000 in healthcare (SHRM; Pin). Watch for: interviewer time — usually the biggest uncounted line item. Full guide with the math: cost per hire.

4. Cost per interview

What: channel spend ÷ interviews that actually happened. The metric almost nobody tracks and the one we'd keep if we could only keep one. Applications are noise (see the funnel above); interviews are the first unit that predicts hires. Formula: channel spend ÷ completed interviews, per channel, per quarter.
Why it matters: it exposes expensive channels instantly. A $500 job board spend producing 2 interviews costs $250/interview. A flat-rate event producing 40 costs a fraction of that. Run last quarter's numbers — the ranking will surprise you.

5. Application-to-interview rate

What: % of applicants who reach an interview. Benchmark: 3% (CareerPlug).
Read it both ways: far below 3% means your screening is a bottleneck or your postings attract the wrong crowd; far above with bad interview outcomes means screening is too loose. The fix is almost always better qualification questions up front — not more resume review hours.

6. Interview-to-hire rate

What: % of interviewed candidates hired. Benchmark: ~27% overall (CareerPlug); single digits for technical roles (Ashby).
Why it matters: low rates mean interviews are being spent on unqualified candidates — a pre-screening failure wearing an interviewing costume.

7. Interview show rate

What: % of scheduled interviews where the candidate appears. Benchmark: ~45% for virtual hiring events per Indeed's published data; hourly/entry-level roles run lower through traditional scheduling. Self-scheduled formats run far higher — JobFairX events average 91%.
The lever: who picked the slot. Self-scheduled interviews — candidate chooses the employer and the time — consistently outperform recruiter-chased scheduling. If you track only one operational metric this quarter, track this.

8. Offer acceptance rate

What: offers accepted ÷ offers extended. Benchmark: 69–92% depending on sector — healthcare sits near the bottom around 77% (NACE; industry data).
Why it matters: every declined offer resets your entire funnel cost. Low acceptance usually means slow process (candidates accepted elsewhere) or a comp surprise late in the game. Speed fixes more offers than money does.

9. Quality of hire

What: how good the hires are — proxy it with 90-day retention plus hiring-manager satisfaction at 90 days. No industry benchmark worth citing; benchmark against yourself, by channel. A channel with cheap hires who quit in 60 days is your most expensive channel.

10. Recruiting ROI

What: value of hires vs. total cost of channel — the metric that rolls the other nine into a budget decision. Formula and business-case template: recruiting ROI.
